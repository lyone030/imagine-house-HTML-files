496 Broome St · SoHo · NYC
Chapter 01 — Heritage

A building with a legend.

The Story

Tucked into SoHo's cast-iron blocks, 496 Broome has long belonged to the city's artistic memory. In the autumn of 1971, John Lennon and Yoko Ono purchased the two-story townhouse from artist David Diao through their company, Ono Music.

It was the first home they owned together in New York. For the two years that followed, the building was a home, workplace, and gathering space. It was a quiet downtown counterpart to the studios uptown, with a recording room clad in purple carpet on the upper floor and a steady current of artists, musicians, and poets through its doors.

The couple moved on to the Dakota in 1973, but the building remained in the family for over fifty years. Today, Imagine House is a private salon for high-quality events. Here, the room does the talking.

A Chronology

The building's arc.

1885

Construction

The two-story cast-iron building is erected on Broome Street, one of dozens of similar commercial houses defining what would become SoHo's architectural identity. Wide-plank floors, twin skylights, 14-foot ceilings.

1960s

The Artist Wave

SoHo's industrial townhouses begin attracting painters, sculptors, and musicians priced out of Greenwich Village. 496 Broome enters the city's creative orbit, passing between artists and becoming a studio and gathering space.

November 1971

Lennon & Ono Arrive

John Lennon and Yoko Ono purchase the townhouse from artist David Diao through their company, Ono Music. It becomes the first home they own together in New York; a downtown sanctuary, recording space and salon in one. The upper floor is fitted with a recording room lined in purple carpet.

1971–1973

Years In Residence

Artists, musicians and poets move through the townhouse in a quiet, steady current. The building functions as home, studio, and gathering place. During this period Lennon records and collaborates; the townhouse becomes a node in the city's creative conversation.

1973

The Dakota & After

Lennon and Ono relocate to the Dakota on Central Park West. 496 Broome remains within the family estate for over fifty years — occupied variously as studio, archive, and private gallery.

Today

Imagine House

The townhouse opens as a private venue for considered gatherings — curated dinners, brand activations, cultural salons and listening rooms. The bones are unchanged: cast-iron columns, skylights, wide-plank floors. The room still does the talking.
